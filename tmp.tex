\begin{abstract}
Medical image archives contain millions of studies with limited structured annotations, making zero-shot, text-based image retrieval a critical yet underexplored capability. Biomedical vision-language models are primarily optimized for report-to-image matching, which fundamentally misaligns with clinical free-text query retrieval and posses biasses from the free-form of writing. Consequently, these models suffer severe degradation when confronted with compositional queries, particularly those involving multi-pathology conjunctions and explicit negations. To expose and address this gap, we introduce \textsc{CXR-Retrieve}, a structured retrieval benchmark %derived from MIMIC-CXR 
containing 5,159 test images and 247 queries spanning both single and composed labels. \mk{are we?} We propose a novel %label-aware
contrastive learning objective that specifically targets free-text retrieval. Our method selectively attracts images with compatible pathology profiles --- including pairs that share confirmed absences --- while withholding attraction from contradictory pairs, naturally separating conflicting clinical concepts such as ``edema'' and ``no edema'' through contrastive competition. By training across all query types, our approach significantly improves retrieval precision over state-of-the-art reference models by \mk{Add number}, setting a new standard for compositional and negation-aware chest radiography retrieval.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Clinical medical archives contain massive volumes of imaging data, yet the vast majority remain unannotated, severely limiting searchable access. Effective zero-shot image retrieval holds profound clinical and research value: it enables rapid dataset construction, supports case-based radiologist education, and powers clinical decision-support tools that surface visually similar prior cases. While vision-language pretrained models (VLPs) like CLIP~\cite{radford2021learning} have demonstrated remarkable success in learning joint image-text embedding spaces, their application to complex medical domains remains constrained.

A primary bottleneck is a fundamental objective mismatch. Most foundational medical VLPs, such as CXR-CLIP~\cite{you2023cxrclip} and BioMed-CLIP~\cite{biomedclip}, are trained almost exclusively under a \textbf{report-matching objective}, aiming to retrieve a study based on its corresponding full radiology report. This is fundamentally distinct from \textbf{clinical query retrieval}, where a user inputs a free-form constraint (e.g., ``pleural effusion but no cardiomegaly'') to retrieve scans satisfying that specific clinical profile. Due to this mismatch, current models exhibit two systematic failure modes. First, \textbf{compositional queries} cause sharp precision degradation, where multi-pathology conjunctions overwhelm the model's standard retrieval capacity \mk{Add ref}. Second, \textbf{negation constraints} effectively fail entirely due to a systemic affirmation bias that incorrectly links negated text tokens to positive visual features.\mk{Add ref to table/figure.. likely good to be to teaser}

To address these critical limitations, this paper makes three key contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Benchmark.} We introduce \textsc{CXR-Retrieve}, a structured retrieval benchmark derived from MIMIC-CXR~\cite{johnson2019mimic} comprising 5,159 images and 247 dynamic queries. It features explicit evaluation tiers for single-label, multi-label conjunction, and negation queries---specifically engineered to expose failure modes missed by traditional report-level metrics.
    
    \item \textbf{Method.} We propose a novel label-aware contrastive objective designed for clinical free-text retrieval. By simulating user queries during training and assigning attraction weights
  proportional to label agreement between pairs, our method selectively attracts text-image pairs with compatible pathology profiles while withholding attraction from contradictory ones, building robustness across single-label, conjunction, and negation query types.
    
    \item \textbf{Analysis.} Extensively evaluated against state-of-the-art baselines including BioMed-CLIP and CXR-CLIP, our approach establishes new benchmarks across single-label, conjunction, and negation query retrieval, proving the necessity of explicitly modeling exclusionary clinical logic.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Benchmark}

MIMIC-CXR~\cite{PhysioNet-mimic-cxr-2.1.0} - 377{,}110 chest
radiographs from 227{,}827 studies, paired only with their free-text reports
and no structured labels --- which is why models trained to match these reports
to images fail when queried for specific pathologies on demand. We build on the
MIMIC-CXR-JPG~\cite{johnson2019mimicjpg} variant, which adds 14 pathology labels
per study extracted from the reports by two NLP labelers: NegBio ~\cite{peng2017negbiohighperformancetoolnegation} and CheXpert~\cite{irvin2019chexpert};
per-label agreement against radiologist annotations is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:f1}. This allows us to train models to match label-based queries to clinical images.
The dataset provides official train/test splits so no patient leakage occurs.
The test set has 5{,}159 images and 247 queries.
We keep 10 of the 14 labels, dropping Support Devices
(not a clinical finding) and No Finding, Enlarged Cardiomediastinum, and
Pleural Other for low labeler precision.

\subsection{Text-to-Image Retrieval with CLIP}
We pre-compute all image embeddings offline. At query time, the model encodes the text query and retrieves the top-$K$ gallery images whose embeddings have the highest cosine similarity with the query embedding. A query is successful if the retrieved image's labels satisfy all constraints of the query.

\subsection{Label Semantics}
Each label takes one of four values: positive (1), negative (0), uncertain
($-1$), or unmentioned (blank). 
This lets us construct query--image pairs over a single label or a composite of several labels, each required to be present or absent.

\subsection{Handling Missing Labels}
A report only states what the radiologist chose to mention; an unmentioned pathology may simply be irrelevant to the case, not confirmed absent.
For evaluation we report two settings: \emph{strict}, counting only explicit negatives (CSV value 0) as absent, and \emph{lenient} (NaN\,=\,absent), which treats unmentioned labels as absent.
For the training label encoding, see the following section.

\subsection{Caption Synthesis}
We synthesize structured free-text captions from CheXpert ~\cite{irvin2019chexpert} label columns to simulate

    \item \textbf{Pair (25\%):} ``a chest X-ray with \textit{A} and \textit{B}''
    \item \textbf{Negation (25\%):} ``a chest X-ray with \textit{A} and no \textit{B}'' (confirmed absent, not merely unmentioned)
\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Single-label:} one required finding (``a chest X-ray with edema'').
    \item \textbf{Conjunction:} two required findings (``edema and effusion'').
    \item \textbf{Negation:} one required, other must be  (``edema but no effusion'').
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
We report three metrics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Precision@$k$:} fraction of the top-$k$ retrieved images that satisfy all constraints of the query.

    \item \textbf{Recall@$k$:} binary indicator equal to 1 if at least one relevant image appears in the top-$k$ results, following standard text-to-image retrieval practice~\cite{radford2021learning}:
    \begin{equation}
        R@k = \frac{1}{|Q|}\sum_{q\in Q}\mathbf{1}\!\left[\mathrm{top\text{-}}k(q)\cap\mathcal{R}_q\neq\varnothing\right],
    \end{equation}
    where $\mathcal{R}_q$ is the set of relevant images for query $q$.

    \item \textbf{Hard Negative Retrieval Rate (HNRR)@$k$:} applies to negation queries only (Tier 3). For a query ``\textit{A} and no \textit{B}'', a \emph{hard negative} is a retrieved image where both pathology $A$ and pathology $B$ are confirmed present (label\,=\,1) — i.e.\ it satisfies the positive constraint while directly violating the negation. HNRR@$k$ is the fraction of the top-$k$ results that are hard negatives, macro-averaged over all negation queries:
    \begin{equation}
        HNRR@k = \frac{1}{|Q|}\sum_{q\in Q}\frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k}\mathbf{1}[\text{image}_i^q \text{ violates negation of } q].
    \end{equation}
    Lower HNRR is better; a value of 0 means no retrieved image ever contradicts the negation constraint.
\end{itemize}




\section{Method}

\subsection{Label-Aware Contrastive Loss}

Each training image carries a label vector $\mathbf{y}_i \in \{-1,0,1\}^L$, where
$1$ = confirmed present, $-1$ = confirmed absent, and $0$ = unmentioned or uncertain.
Let $\mathbf{z}_i^v, \mathbf{z}_i^t\in\mathbb{R}^d$ be the $\ell_2$-normalised image
and text embeddings, and $\tau$ the learned logit scale.

\paragraph{Label kernel.}
We measure the semantic similarity of two samples by the dot product of their label
vectors:
\begin{equation}
    K(i,j) = \mathbf{y}_i^\top\mathbf{y}_j
    = \underbrace{|\{l:y_{il}=y_{jl}=1\}|}_{\text{shared positives}}
    + \underbrace{|\{l:y_{il}=y_{jl}=-1\}|}_{\text{shared negatives}}
    - \underbrace{|\{l:y_{il}{\cdot}y_{jl}=-1\}|}_{\text{conflicts}}.
    \label{eq:kernel}
\end{equation}
$K(i,j)>0$ means the images agree on which findings are present and absent;
$K(i,j)<0$ flags a clinical contradiction.
We clamp $K(i,i)\leftarrow\max(K(i,i),1)$ so every image is always a positive for itself.

\paragraph{Soft target and loss.}
Negative-$K$ entries are zeroed and each row is normalised to form a target
distribution:
\begin{equation}
    T_{ij} = \frac{\max(K(i,j),\,0)}{\sum_{k}\max(K(i,k),\,0)},
    \label{eq:target}
\end{equation}
which is used as the target for the standard symmetric CLIP cross-entropy:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{2B}\sum_{i}\Biggl[
        \sum_{j} T_{ij}\log\frac{e^{\tau\langle\mathbf{z}_i^v,\mathbf{z}_j^t\rangle}}
                                {\sum_{k}e^{\tau\langle\mathbf{z}_i^v,\mathbf{z}_k^t\rangle}}
      + \sum_{j} T_{ji}\log\frac{e^{\tau\langle\mathbf{z}_i^t,\mathbf{z}_j^v\rangle}}
                                {\sum_{k}e^{\tau\langle\mathbf{z}_i^t,\mathbf{z}_k^v\rangle}}
    \Biggr].
    \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
When $T$ is the identity this reduces to standard CLIP.
Pairs with $T_{ij}>0$ attract in proportion to label agreement. All other pairs --- including clinically conflicting ones ($K(i,j)<0$) --- receive $T_{ij}=0$ and are neither attracted nor explicitly repelled; they enter the softmax denominator identically to any standard negative pair.

\paragraph{Treating unmentioned labels as missing.}
Radiologists document only salient findings; $81\%$ of label slots in MIMIC-CXR are
unmentioned.  Encoding these as absent ($-1$) would make nearly every batch pair
appear to conflict --- an image with confirmed edema conflicts with any image that
simply never mentions edema --- flooding $K$ with spurious negative signal.
We therefore encode unmentioned labels as $0$, so only the $4.7\%$ of slots carrying
a confirmed absence contribute to $K$, restricting repulsion to genuine clinical
contradictions.


\section{implementation details}

\subsection{Architecture}

Dual-encoder (ViT-B/32 vision + CLIP text encoder), fine-tuned via LoRA adapters on
attention and MLP layers. Only $\sim$1.9\% of parameters are trainable; adapters are
merged into base weights after training.

\subsection{Hyperparameters}
\paragraph{LoRA.}
We apply LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora} to the attention projection layers (\texttt{in\_proj}, \texttt{out\_proj}) and MLP layers (\texttt{c\_fc}, \texttt{c\_proj}) of the ViT-B/32 text and vision encoders, with rank $r{=}16$, scaling $\alpha{=}16$ (effective scale $\alpha/r = 1$), and dropout $0.05$. This yields 2.95M trainable out of 154.2M total parameters (1.91\%).

\paragraph{Optimisation.}
We train with AdamW, learning rate $10^{-4}$, cosine decay to $10^{-8}$, linear warmup over 5 epochs, and early stopping with patience 10 (no improvement on validation loss). Effective batch size is 180 (90 per GPU $\times$ 2 GPUs) with bfloat16 precision.

\paragraph{Training captions.}
At each step, a caption is sampled per image: 50\% single-label (``\textit{atelectasis}''), 25\% pair (``\textit{atelectasis and edema}''), 25\% negation (``\textit{atelectasis and no edema}''). Negation captions require a confirmed-absent label (CSV value 0); unmentioned labels (NaN) are treated as unknown and excluded from both the caption and the label kernel $K$.

\paragraph{Image preprocessing.}
Images are resized to $224\times224$ and normalised with the OpenAI CLIP statistics (mean $[0.481, 0.458, 0.408]$, std $[0.269, 0.261, 0.276]$).
